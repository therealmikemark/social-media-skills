# High-CTR thumbnail principles

The craft rules every concept must satisfy. These are necessary but not
sufficient — strategy (see desire-loop.md) and differentiation (see the research
step) are what make a thumbnail *clicked*, these are what make it *readable*.

## Composition
- **Face fills 30-50% of the frame.** A clear human face is the strongest CTR
  driver. Never hide the face.
- **One clear focal element besides the face** — a bold number, logo, arrow,
  prop, or before/after. Not three competing elements.
- **Rule of thirds.** Face on one side, text/payoff on the other usually beats
  centred everything.
- **Readable at 320px wide** (YouTube mobile). Squint-test: if you can't parse it
  squinting, it fails.

## Text
- **3-5 words maximum.** 3 is best, 5 is the ceiling, 6 only if unavoidable.
- **It is a hook phrase, not a sentence.** "I fired my team", "It's not cardio",
  "Don't do this".
- **Large, bold, sans-serif**, with a contrasting outline or shadow so it survives
  any background.
- **Never bottom-right** — the watch-time timestamp sits there.

## Colour & contrast
- **Two dominant colours**: brand primary + one high-contrast accent (yellow, red,
  cyan, teal all pull well).
- **High contrast** between face, text, and background. Test by squinting.
- Use the research step's finding to *break the pattern* — if competitors are all
  bright/white, go dark cinematic, and vice versa. Standing out beats looking good.

## Emotion
- The expression carries the pain or payoff. Exaggerate slightly — wide eyes,
  open mouth, knowing smirk, intense focus. Neutral faces underperform.
- Match the expression to the Desire Loop pain/outcome, not a generic "smile".

## Brand guards (Coaching Sales)
- **No em dashes** anywhere in text.
- **British English** in any prose unless voice.md says otherwise. (Thumbnail
  hook text follows the title's language.)
- If `brand-kit.md` exists in the project, use its exact hex colours.
- Keep a **consistent style across videos** for channel recognition — same face
  treatment, same font family, same accent logic.

## Anti-patterns
- More than 6 words of text.
- Tiny logos or small print.
- Three-plus focal elements fighting for attention.
- Low contrast / muddy mid-tones.
- A face that is small, turned away, or hidden.
- Spelling out the answer (closes the curiosity gap, kills the click).
